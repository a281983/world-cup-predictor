01Purpose & what it is not

This is an interactive what-if tool, not a calibrated forecaster. You set a theory of what wins football tournaments — how much weight to put on history vs. current form vs. your own gut and your tolerance for chaos — and the engine re-scores all 48 teams, completes the group stage, seeds the knockouts, and simulates through to the title under your assumptions.

What it is not: there is no fitted/trained model and no betting edge. The default weights are sensible priors chosen by hand, not coefficients estimated from historical outcomes. Treat every number as "what your theory implies," not "what will happen."

02Data sources

  • Match structure & real results — the fixtures, the group results played so far, and the full knockout wiring come from the open-source openfootball/worldcup.json dataset (MIT licence). The live-refresh button re-reads this same feed in the browser.
  • FIFA rank & Elo — Elo is computed, not estimated: the World Football Elo method (K-factor by match importance, margin-of-victory multiplier, home advantage) run over the full ~49,000-match history in martj42/international_results (CC0), through the most recent fixture in the dataset. FIFA rank is derived from that Elo. Elo is the dominant predictor by default.
  • World Cup pedigree — titles / finals / semi-finals are the real historical record.
  • Head-to-head records — computed from martj42/international_results (CC0), ~49,000 international matches since 1872. For each pair we take the last ten meetings, recency-weighted.
  • Recent form — derived directly from the real played results at this tournament.
  • Squad & tactical fields — synthetic, generated deterministically from each team's Elo with a small seeded jitter. Plausible and illustrative, not measured.

04Feature engineering & standardisation

Eleven features feed the model. Three are composed first:

  • Pedigree = 3·titles + 1.5·finals + 1·semis
  • GF ÷ GA = (goalsFor + 1) / (goalsAgainst + 1) (the +1 avoids divide-by-zero)
  • Form = points-per-game from the last five results, scaled to 0–1

The features live on wildly different scales (Elo ≈ 1500–2100; titles 0–5; possession 40–60%). Adding them directly would let the big-numbered ones dominate by accident. So every feature is converted to a z-score — how many standard deviations a team sits above or below the 48-team average — and clamped to ±3 so a single outlier can't blow up the model:

zᵢ = ( xᵢ − μ ) / σ   clamped to [−3, +3]

Standardisation is the standard, correct preparation for combining heterogeneous predictors: after it, a one-unit change means the same thing ("one standard deviation") for every feature.

Earlier versions averaged 0–1 min-max values. Averaging compresses teams toward the middle, which is exactly why moving a slider barely changed the bracket. Z-scores preserve the real spread, so reweighting actually moves teams.

05The scoring model — team "strength"

Each team gets a single strength number: a weighted sum of its standardised features. The sliders are the weights. Features are grouped into three buckets, each bucket summed, then the buckets combined by their master weights:

strength = m₁·Σ(w·z)bucket1 + m₂·Σ(w·z)bucket2 + m₃·Σ(w·z)bucket3

where w are the sub-sliders (Elo, form, pedigree…) and m are the three bucket master sliders. This is a linear predictor — structurally identical to the linear term inside a logistic regression, but with weights you set by hand instead of weights fitted from data. Summing (not averaging) keeps the spread, so a team that is +2σ on Elo and +1.5σ on form ends up clearly ahead, and shifting weight between buckets genuinely reshuffles the order.

Four context terms are added when two specific teams meet:

  • Head-to-head — the two teams' real historical record (last ten meetings, recency-weighted to a number in −1…+1), scaled by the H2H slider. Zero when they have never met.
  • Home / CONCACAF edge — a bonus for hosts USA, Mexico, Canada, scaled by its slider.
  • Gut-pick boost — up to a large fixed bump for your hand-picked team.
  • Penalty record — added only in knockout ties, scaled by the penalty slider.

06The win-probability model

Given two strengths, the probability that A beats B is the logistic function of their difference — the Bradley–Terry model for paired comparisons:

P(A beats B) = 1 / ( 1 + e −k·(strengthₐ − strength_b) )

The Upset Sensitivity slider sets the temperature k:

k = 0.22 + 0.85·(1 − upset)

Low upset → large k → steep curve → favourites win decisively ("chalk"). High upset → small k → flat curve → every game drifts toward a coin-flip ("chaos"). The strength ordering never changes with k; only how confidently it is expressed does — which is why chaos widens the title odds without making nonsense favourites.

07Group-stage completion

Played games (real scores) are locked. Each unplayed group game is resolved from its win probability: within a narrow band (|p − 0.5| < 0.06) it is called a draw, otherwise the favourite takes it by a one- or two-goal margin depending on how lopsided p is. Tables are then ranked on points → goal difference → goals for → Elo, giving each group's winner, runner-up and third place.

08Knockout seeding (the tricky part)

In the 48-team format, the eight best third-placed teams advance. They are ranked across all twelve groups (points → GD → GF → Elo) and the top eight qualify. Each Round-of-32 slot for a third-placed team lists which groups are eligible (e.g. 3A/B/C/D/F). Assigning the eight qualifiers to the eight slots so every eligibility rule holds is a constraint-satisfaction problem, solved here by backtracking search (slots with fewest options filled first). This reproduces FIFA's official allocation table rather than approximating it.

09Knockout simulation

The bracket is resolved in match-number order, 73 → 104, following the real wiring. Each W74 reference reads the stored winner of match 74; the third-place game (103) reads the two losing semi-finalists via L101 / L102. For the on-screen bracket each tie is decided deterministically (higher win probability advances), which is why it is stable and readable.

10Monte-Carlo title odds

The single deterministic bracket can't express uncertainty, so the knockout is re-simulated about 1,800 times. Each run is stochastic: every tie is settled by a random draw against its probability (a seeded RNG keeps it reproducible). A team's title odds = the fraction of runs it wins. Seeding is fixed before the simulations for speed and a stable display bracket.

Path vs. marginal — a real subtlety. The headline champion is the winner of the single most-likely bracket; the title-odds leaderboard counts wins across all random brackets. These can disagree: a team can be favoured on the one most-likely path while another team wins more often across the full space of paths. Both are correct answers to different questions.

11Razor-edge sensitivity

Any knockout tie closer than roughly 60/40 (|p − 0.5| < 0.12) is flagged with an orange dot: a genuine coin-flip that a small change in your assumptions could swing. Robust results get a green dot.

12Is this the right model, not just a working one?

For the job it actually has — let a user dial in a theory and see a coherent, interpretable bracket — the standardised-linear-predictor-plus-logistic design is the appropriate choice, for concrete reasons:

  • Standardisation is the textbook way to combine features on different scales.
  • A weighted sum of standardised features is the linear predictor at the heart of generalised linear models — transparent and monotone, so each slider has a clear, explainable effect.
  • The logistic link is the standard model for a binary outcome (win/lose) and gives the Bradley–Terry comparison used throughout sports rating.
  • A single temperature cleanly separates "who is stronger" from "how often the stronger team actually wins," which is exactly the chalk-vs-chaos control the tool needs.

The honestly "best" model would fit the weights — e.g. estimate logistic- regression coefficients (or a Dixon–Coles bivariate-Poisson goal model) from decades of international results, and report calibration (Brier score, reliability curves). That is deliberately out of scope: the whole point is that you own the weights. Fitting them would replace your theory with the data's, and add machinery a one-page toy doesn't need. So: the same form as the best model, with hand-set rather than fitted coefficients — the right trade for this task.

13Limitations & honest caveats

  • No coefficients are fitted or back-tested; odds are internally consistent but not calibrated.
  • Elo and head-to-head are computed from real results, but the squad/tactical inputs (depth, cohesion, availability, penalty record) remain synthetic, Elo-anchored placeholders — they add interactivity more than independent signal, because no free, account-free source provides them.
  • Group games are simulated with a crude scoreline rule, not a full goal model.
  • Head-to-head is real but uneven: about 75% of team pairs have prior meetings (45% have three or more); the rest are treated as neutral.
